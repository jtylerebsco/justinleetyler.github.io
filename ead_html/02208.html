Biographical/Historical note

Born in Grodno, Poland on March 16, 1903, Louis Redstone (born Leon Rotenstein) left home at the age of seventeen to immigrate to Palestine. As a member of the Zionist Pioneer Youth Group, he spent three years planting trees and learning the construction trade. He came to Detroit in the 1920s, where his brother Sol was already living, and got a job as a bricklayer on Detroit's Buhl Building. Redstone enrolled in the University of Michigan School of Architecture, graduating in 1929. He worked for a time for Albert Kahn Associates, but returned to Palestine in 1933 where he opened his first architecture practice in Tel Aviv, designing apartments. In 1937 he returned to Detroit and formed his own company, Louis G. Redstone & Associates. Redstone later attended Cranbrook Academy of Art, where he graduated with an advanced degree in urban planning. He studied under the direction of noted Finnish architect Eliel Saarinen, for whom he named his youngest son. Redstone married Ruth Rosenbaum, a teacher and social worker from New York, in June 1939. They had two sons, Daniel and Eliel, both of whom received a degree in architecture from U of M. Daniel joined his father's firm; Eliel formed his own architectural firm in San Francisco. Louis Redstone died in Detroit on March 30, 2002, at the age of ninety-nine.

Series I: Architectural Career 

Scope and Summary note

Louis Redstone designed buildings throughout the metropolitan Detroit area, including the International Terminal at Detroit Metropolitan Airport, the Jewish Community Center in West Bloomfield, shopping centers such as Somerset Collection South and numerous banks, libraries and schools. Among his works was the Detroit Public Library's Chaney Branch.

Series II: Artistic Career (see also LMS) 

Scope and Summary note

A talented artist, Louis Redstone was a watercolor painter and sculptor. Many of his works—some originals, others in print form—are contained in this series. Mr. Redstone exhibited his work, and programs and photographs from some of these exhibits may be found here. He was also a writer, and, with his wife, Ruth, authored eight books.

Series IV: Personal Life 

Scope and Summary note

Louis Redstone received a number of awards and honors in his lifetime, for his professional work as well as his religious and philanthropic interests. This section contains these materials, as well as celebrations, personal papers and photographs. Photos include pictures of the Redstone home on Appoline Street in Detroit. Built in 1940, it was a beautiful brick house with a backyard that featured original sculptures and a built-in wading pool. Family members include his sons Daniel and Eliel; his grandson Adam, born in 1982; granddaughter Carly, born in 1984; his wife Ruth; and her family, the Rosenbaum's. Ruth Rosenbaum Redstone was born on March 21, 1906 in Ogdensburg, New York. As a social worker, she came to Detroit in the early 1930s to work for the Jewish Family and Children's Service. Active in many Jewish organizations, Mrs. Redstone volunteered for a number of worthy causes. She received the United Fund Heart of Gold Award in 1974 for her volunteer efforts. At the age of sixty-nine, Mrs. Redstone joined a synchronized swimming team, and in 1975 her team won awards from the National Masters Synchronized Swimming Championship. After Mr. Redstone's death in 2002, Mrs. Redstone sold the house on Appolinc and moved into a senior housing development in Southfield. By the time of her 101 birthday in 2006, she was no longer swimming, but had taken up Tai Chi and Yoga, and spent her birthday "dancing the night away!"
