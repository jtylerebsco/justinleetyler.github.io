Biographical/Historical note

The first zoological park in Detroit was established in the early 1880s, and was the result of a small traveling circus that went bankrupt. The citizens of the City of Detroit took up a collection to assist in housing and feeding the abandoned creatures, and the Detroit Zoological Gardens, located on the site that would later become Tiger Stadium, was opened to the public on September 5, 1883. The park suffered from financial problems, however, and by July 1884 was closed. It was reported that the public took many of the animals home with them; the few remaining animals were turned over to the City of Detroit and the Belle Isle Zoo was established to house them. By 1901, the Belle Isle Zoo undertook a serious building program, with the Aquarium, Deer Shelter, Bear Dens and other exhibits erected. The Belle Isle Aquarium, the most ambitious of the exhibits, was opened to the public on August 18, 1904. The Detroit Zoological Society was formed in July 1911 by three prominent Detroiters; over the next few years, more men joined the group, but it took over a decade before the establishment of a Zoological Park Commission. In 1916, one hundred acres of the Hendrie Farm was purchased by the Society, located between Ten and Eleven Mile Roads, west of Woodward, in Royal Oak. Mayor James Couzens encouraged voters to accept the gift of the property by the Society, with the added incentive that an anonymous friend would agree to give the City an additional 160 acres of land adjoining the property. The gift was finally accepted in April of 1923, and a year later Mayor John C. Lodge established the Detroit Zoological Commission. Of the four men appointed to the CommissiOn, noted attorney Horace H. Rackham had been the donor of the additional acreage, which resulted in the Rackham Golf Course, next to the Detroit Zoo. Others on the Commission were attorney Henry Ledyard, Walter 0. Briggs, industrialist, and James S. Holden, a real estate tycoon who was to remain involved in the Zoo's operation for the next forty-three years (see Box 5:1). Under these men, the Detroit Zoo expanded its exhibits and additional structures such as the elegant Bird House, the rugged Bear Dens, and deliberately un-landscaped Cloverleaf Lake. The Detroit Zoo was the first modern zoo to feature bar-less exhibits throughout the grounds. Opening day for the Detroit Zoological Park in Royal Oak was August 1, 1928; over four thousand persons visited the Zoo in the first hour. Originally run by the Parks and Recreation Department, in 1941 the operation of the Belle Isle Zoo and Aquarium was transferred to the Zoological Park Commission. making it ancillary to the Detroit Zoological Park. This collection provides a fascinating behind-the-scenes look at a major metropolitan zoo. It deals with all aspects of the Detroit Zoo, including operating procedures, training programs, purchases of animals, zoo construction, landscaping and maintenance. For example, Tree Moving was a major project in the 1930s, as a Work Progress Administration (WPA) project. Thousands of trees from farms throughout the metropolitan area were moved and planted in Royal Oak to provide a park atmosphere for the new zoo (Box 11-12). Materials are arranged alphabetically by subject.
