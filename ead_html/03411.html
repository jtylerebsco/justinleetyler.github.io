Biographical/Historical note

Goodwill Industries was founded by Boston, Massachusetts philanthropist Henry Morgan in 1905 as the Morgan Memorial Cooperative Industries and Store, Inc. After several name changes, the organization was absorbed by the Board of Home Missions of the Methodist Episcopal Church in Philadelphia and became the Bureau of Goodwill Industries. In 1934, it became the National Association for Goodwill Industries and operated under that name for 48 years. In 1971, Goodwill Industries merged with the League for Handicapped Services and now goes by the official name of Goodwill Industries-League for the Handicapped Services. The organization's mission is to help the plight of the urban poor and the handicapped of America. It collects old clothes, furniture, and appliances. They hire handicapped workers to clean and repair the equipment and then sell it to the poor in their Goodwill stores. It's motto is "not a charity, but a chance." In 1907, the first Women's Auxiliary was founded to support Henry Morgan's work in Boston. In 1930, the National Women's Auxiliary to Goodwill Industries was organized. The women's auxiliaries or associations (as they were known) worked to raise money for Goodwill Industries and promote the organization through community events such as luncheons, teas, dinner and other entertainment. The associations worked closely with the Protestant churches in their communities. A Junior Auxiliary group was created for younger women and also worked to support both organizations. The Detroit chapter of Goodwill Industries began in 1921 and was officially chartered by the national association in 1926. It was still in existence as of July 1995. The Women's Association of Goodwill Industries in Detroit was organized in 1931. Mrs. John H. Stalker was the first chapter president and there were 116 members. They worked to support Goodwill Industries in Detroit by promoting Goodwill within the Protestant Community, by collecting material for the Goodwill Stores, and by providing emergency food and medical supplies. The Women's Association in Detroit was active until 1989. Kathryn G. Leonard was the last association president. The papers of the Women's Association of Goodwill Industries in Detroit reflect their work to support the charitable works of the Detroit Goodwill Industries and to a lesser extent, Goodwill Industries of America.

Subseries A: Agendas, Minutes and Reports 

Scope and Contents note

Meeting agendas, minutes and officer reports; includes material from both monthly association meetings and executive board meetings. The documentation from 1948-1953, 1957, 1960-1962 and 1984-1989 is incomplete. Agendas are only included from 1977-1983. The social service officer reports from 1935-1946 are well documented. Files are arranged chronologically.
