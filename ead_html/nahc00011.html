Biographical/Historical note

Perrin began in 1898 as a draftsman for Lozier Bicycle in Toledo, Ohio. He, along with George Burwell, built a steam car and Rochet-type tricycle using a De Dion gasoline engine. To learn more about automobiles, Perrin spent six months on the East coast selling and repairing foreign cars. The Lozier Motor Company was originally established in Plattsburgh, New York to manufacture marine engines and launches. Perrin became Chief Engineer and Manufacturing Head of Lozier Motor Co. Eventually, he was in charge of the Detroit, Michigan plant, which was built in 1910, to manufacture automobiles. In its heyday, the Lozier Motor Company produced upscale automobiles, some selling for as much as $7,000. They were modeled after foreign cars and were known for their stamina and durability. In 1911, a Lozier automobile won the Vanderbilt Cup race. Due to rapid expansion, the Lozier Motor Company went into receivership in 1914. Perrin left the company in 1915. Perrin briefly worked as Chief Engineer at the Timken-Detroit Axle Company, before leaving to work at Signal Corps, U.S. Army, developing aircraft. While at Signal Corps, a report of Perrin's was turned in to the subcommittee of the Committee on Military Affairs, U.S. Senate. This report detailed the problems with Bristol fighting planes, which were almost adopted for production by the committee. As a result of Perrin's report, the Liberty 12 engine was used. He expressed that the problems adapting the Bristol were caused by weight design problems, poor materials, and a failure to have experienced and qualified men engaged in the work. This work with aircraft engines led him to Willys-Overland, Limited, in Toronto, Canada, where he was contracted to produce 2,000 Sunbeam "Arab" engines in one year. Because of serious design problems, only 123 engines were shipped to Great Britain. After the World War I Armistice, Perrin prepared Willys-Overland, Limited for production of a new Model 4 car. Perrin left Willys-Overland, Limited to help Stevens-Duryea, Inc. correct automobile mechanical problems, but became involved in a reorganization scheme. Subsequently, Perrin worked for approximately ten years as a consulting engineer focusing on single valve air cooled engines. Later, he joined Pratt and Whitney Aircraft. Upon retirement, he was on faculty at Trinity College in Hartford, Connecticut, teaching engineering. Throughout his life, Perrin was active in many associations, particularly, the Society of Automotive Engineers. He was a charter member in 1904 and the first vice-president in 1913. He received a lifetime membership in 1940. John G. Perrin's contributions to the automotive and aircraft industries were many. He held seven patents, including a tire carrier patent, a combination tool-box and gas-tank holder patent, a hood for automobiles patent, and an automobile side lamp patent. He was the first to use a shaft drive in a high powered car and the first to design a curved fender on a Michigan-built car.

Provenance note

The papers of John George Perrin were purchased from the American Society of Body Engineers and placed in the National Automotive History Collection of the Detroit Public Library on October 30, 1967. The John G. Perrin Collection was opened for research in March of 1994.
